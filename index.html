01 — Research

Proof of Concept: The Hard Way First

UberDuck ElevenLabs API Testing

I didn't know if what I wanted to build was technically feasible, so I didn't design a pixel before I had a working prototype. I used Claude to research the current landscape of music generation AI, surfacing early candidates: UberDuck and Kits.ai.

UberDuck's fast voice cloning worked on their web interface. But the API required significantly longer samples than the web app. That asymmetry would've been a fatal UX problem — users shouldn't need to record several minutes of audio just to hear a quick demo.

I pivoted to ElevenLabs — the industry leader in text-to-speech. It worked: I got an AI voice clone rapping my lyrics. It sounded terrible. Not because the clone was poor — it actually sounded like me — but because text-to-speech models and rap performance are fundamentally incompatible.

The prototype worked technically. The output failed artistically. That distinction mattered.

02 — Insight

User Research Method: Interviewing Myself

Role Separation Behavioral Research

Rather than assuming I knew what problem the tool should solve, I used Claude to conduct structured user research — on me. If I wrote the interview questions myself and then answered them myself, my researcher brain and my user brain would contaminate each other.

I gave Claude the role of UX researcher and gave myself permission to just be the user. The AI asked the questions. I answered honestly, without steering toward conclusions I'd already reached.

The research reframed the product entirely. I didn't need a voice cloning tool. I needed an AI session artist. HEARD would own steps 1–3. The user owns step 4.

03 — Technology

Why Diffusion Models Changed Everything

Suno / Kie AI Architecture Decision

While testing, I found tools producing significantly better musical output. The difference in quality was stark, and I wanted to understand why.

ElevenLabs and UberDuck are text-to-speech models — trained on spoken dialogue and audiobooks. They understand emotional pacing and vocal tone, but have no concept of bars, beat structure, or musical timing. They produce emotional speech, not musical performance.

Diffusion models are different. They're trained on actual songs, including instrumentals. They understand the relationship between a lyric and a beat. They produce performances that feel musical because they were shaped by music.

You can't make a speech engine understand rap timing. Once I understood that, the path was clear. I rebuilt the prototype around Suno via the Kie AI service — low per-use cost, output quality in a different class.

04 — Usability

Genre Literacy: The Real UX Friction

Usability Study AI-Assisted Research

I ran an AI-assisted usability study — Claude prompted me with questions as I moved through each step of the actual prototype. The goal was to catch the issues that felt invisible to me as the builder.

The most significant finding: genre literacy. The prototype listed 16 genres as a wall of checkboxes. For a new user, those labels meant almost nothing in terms of predicting what the final output would actually sound like.

If users can't predict the output, they can't make good decisions. And if they can't make good decisions, they waste credits and lose trust in the tool.

The Fixes

Added audio samples and written descriptions to each genre. Expanded and organized options into subcategories. Introduced AI-generated prompts feeding into the music model to improve output quality.

05 — Brand

Naming, Logo, and an Identity That Meant Something

Naming Strategy Visual Identity Figma

A functional product and a product worth using are not the same thing. Brand is the foundation from which all downstream decisions flow — how you articulate your values, establish a voice, and give users something to connect with that isn't just features and pricing.

I used AI to run a structured brand development session. The exercise wasn't about generating a brand — it was about drawing out what I already believed and making it explicit.

The leading name candidates were "First Listen" and "Herd." I originally leaned toward Herd — an intentional alternate spelling, the acronym landed: Hear Every Rhyme Delivered. But days later, the word's other meaning surfaced — cattle, crowds, following — wrong for a product about individual creative expression.

I adjusted to HEARD: Hear Every Authored Rhyme Delivered. Past tense, action completed. A lyricist finally heard.

For the logo: image generation AI couldn't produce final assets, but the process wasn't wasted. The generations gave me directional clarity I wouldn't have found on a blank canvas. I moved into Figma and built the final logo from scratch.

06 — Build

From Prototype to Production Web App

React + Vite FastAPI Claude Code Cursor

Enough design was done to begin real development. I set up a local environment using Cursor and Claude Code. The Figma-to-Claude MCP connection let me translate designs directly into code — the one-shot output got me farther than expected, but like every one-shot prompt, it needed refinement.

Building the signup flow pushed me into product strategy territory. I wanted people to feel like they were joining something — not just creating another account. The flow: homepage → request form → personal review by me on weekends → invite code via personal email → account creation → warm welcome → Step 1.

Together, Claude and I built something that neither of us could have produced alone. Claude couldn't have anticipated the product scenarios. I couldn't have written the implementation. The collaboration was genuine — not me directing a tool, but two different kinds of intelligence working on the same problem.

— Takeaways

What I learned building HEARD

01

AI amplifies ability. It doesn't replace it.

Every tool I used required judgment at every step. Output quality was entirely dependent on the inputs — and those inputs came from 15 years of design experience.

02

The research methods transferred.

AI-assisted self-interview and usability study weren't substitutes for real user research — they were genuine methodological moves. The key was role separation.

03

Designers can ship.

With AI handling the implementation layer, the bottleneck shifted: it was no longer about who could write the code. It was about who knew clearly enough what should happen.

04

Apps are commodities. Brands are not.

Anyone can generate a lyrics-to-music tool right now. What can't be generated: a product that feels made by someone who understands the problem from the inside.

05

Deferred decisions are decisions too.

Voice cloning was my original idea. I cut it. Knowing what to leave out — and being honest about why — is as important as knowing what to build.
